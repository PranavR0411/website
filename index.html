How do you measure the universe with light you can barely see?

Upcoming surveys will photograph billions of galaxies — far too many to ever take a spectrum of each one. All we get per galaxy is a handful of brightness measurements through different filters. This thesis builds a probabilistic machine that turns these measurements into actual spectral energy distributions (SED) and physics: a per-galaxy model of Intergalactic absorption (VILA), a noise-aware population model of galaxy spectra, and a photometric redshift pipeline — all tested on 419,654 selected galaxies from the COSMOS2020 catalogue.

The whole thesis, in ninety seconds

A galaxy's light carries its life story — how many stars it formed, how dusty it is, how far away it sits. A spectrograph can read that story letter by letter, but only for one object at a time. A camera with filters reads it as a blur, seeing entire words/sentences as one. The central question of this work is how much of the story survives the blur — and the answer turns out to be: a lot, if you model everything that happens to the light on its way to us.

Three things happen to that light. The expansion of the universe stretches it redward (that's redshift, the quantity we're after). Dust inside the galaxy reddens it. The clouds of neutral hydrogen scattered across billions of light-years of intergalactic space absorb its blue end, carving the famous "Lyman break" into the spectrum. Crucially, every galaxy looks through a different arrangement of these clouds, which needs to be accounted for.

Most models average over this variation in absorption. Instead a Monte carlo approach is used to overcome this, and the framework is propagated through the whole distribution through population inference and redshift estimation. The result is a framework where the messiness of the real universe is a modelled ingredient, not an inconvenience.

The pipeline at a glance From raw catalogue to cosmology-ready inference

Two streams meet in the middle: real photometry (top) and a compressed, physical model of what galaxies can look like (bottom). VILA welds them together by asking, for every single galaxy, which random arrangements of hydrogen clouds are consistent with what we actually observed. The entrie framework is then applied to get a population model and infer redshifts of galaxies.
